# Retrieval Practice + Spaced Repetition — Theoretical Foundation

## Overview

Retrieval practice is the strategy of recalling information from memory — actively pulling it out — rather than passively reviewing it. When combined with spaced repetition (distributing practice over increasing intervals), it produces the most durable, transferable learning cognitive science has identified.

---

## Core Science

### The Testing Effect
Retrieving information **strengthens** memory more than re-reading, highlighting, or re-listening.

**Key finding (Karpicke & Roediger, 2008):** Retrieval students recalled 80% after one week; re-readers recalled 36%.

Retrieval works even when initial attempts fail — the struggle itself strengthens encoding.

### The Forgetting Curve (Ebbinghaus, 1885)
- 20 min: ~42% forgotten
- 1 hour: ~56% forgotten
- 1 day: ~67% forgotten
- 1 week: ~75% forgotten

Each successful retrieval resets the curve and slows decay.

### The Spacing Effect
Distributing practice over time dramatically beats cramming for long-term retention.

**Recommended schedule:** 1-3-7-21-60 days between reviews. Adaptive algorithms (Anki, SuperMemo) optimize per item.

## Retrieval Techniques

### 1. Free Recall
Close the book, write everything you remember. Compare to source.

### 2. Practice Testing
Answer questions — self-generated or provided. Pre-testing (before studying) primes the brain.

### 3. Flashcards (Done Right)
- One idea per card
- Ask "why" and "how," not just "what"
- Write in your own words
- Include "when would you use this?" questions

### 4. Elaborative Interrogation
"Why is this true?" / "How does this connect to what I already know?"

### 5. Interleaving
Mix different topics/problem types. ABCBCAACB beats AAABBBCCC. Feels harder, produces better results.

---

## Desirable Difficulties (Bjork & Bjork)

Conditions that feel harder but produce better long-term retention:
1. Spacing (vs. massing)
2. Interleaving (vs. blocking)
3. Retrieval practice (vs. re-reading)
4. Varied practice contexts
5. Generation (vs. passive reception)

**Not all difficulty is desirable.** Only difficulty that triggers effective encoding strategies.

---

## Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "It doesn't feel like learning" | Harder feeling = more effective |
| "I need to re-read until I understand" | Understanding comes from retrieval attempts |
| "Flashcards are for memorization only" | Well-designed cards test comprehension and application |
| "Spacing wastes time" | Same total time, dramatically better results |
| "Forgetting is the enemy" | Forgetting creates desirable difficulty |
| "Testing = assessment" | Low-stakes testing is a LEARNING strategy |

---

## Feedback Principles

1. Immediate feedback after each attempt
2. Correct the error — show right answer
3. Explain why — connect to principles
4. Slight delay for confident errors (hypercorrection effect)
