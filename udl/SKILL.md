# Universal Design for Learning (UDL) Skill

> "Disability is not in the person; it is in the interaction between the person and the environment. UDL designs the environment to fit the person." — CAST

## Overview

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) is a framework developed by CAST (Center for Applied Special Technology) that guides the design of learning experiences to proactively address the variability of ALL learners. Inspired by universal design in architecture (curb cuts help everyone, not just wheelchair users), UDL designs curriculum that is flexible and accessible from the start — eliminating the need for most individual retrofits and accommodations.

The core insight: there is no "average" learner. Variability is the norm, not the exception. A curriculum designed for the mythical average student fails most students.

---

## Three Principles of UDL

UDL is organized around three brain networks and their corresponding principles:

| Brain Network | Principle | The "Why," "What," or "How" of Learning |
|---------------|-----------|------------------------------------------|
| **Affective networks** | Multiple Means of Engagement | The WHY of learning — motivation, persistence, self-regulation |
| **Recognition networks** | Multiple Means of Representation | The WHAT of learning — perceiving and comprehending information |
| **Strategic networks** | Multiple Means of Action & Expression | The HOW of learning — navigating and demonstrating knowledge |

---

## Principle 1: Multiple Means of Engagement (The WHY)

Learners differ in what motivates them, what sustains their effort, and how they self-regulate.

### Guideline 1: Recruiting Interest
Provide options for how learners engage with content initially.

**Checkpoints:**
- **Optimize individual choice and autonomy** — Let learners choose topics, tools, sequence, or pace when possible
- **Optimize relevance, value, and authenticity** — Connect content to learners' lives, cultures, and interests
- **Minimize threats and distractions** — Create psychological safety; vary sensory stimulation; establish predictable routines

**Practical examples:**
- Offer a choice board with 4 ways to explore the same concept
- Let students select which problems to solve (from a set that all address the same skill)
- Begin with a real-world problem students care about
- Allow flexible seating and workspace options

### Guideline 2: Sustaining Effort and Persistence
Provide options for maintaining focus and determination.

**Checkpoints:**
- **Heighten salience of goals and objectives** — Make learning targets visible and revisitable
- **Vary demands and resources to optimize challenge** — Adjust difficulty, provide scaffolds that can be removed
- **Foster collaboration and community** — Use peer support, group work, cooperative learning
- **Increase mastery-oriented feedback** — Emphasize growth, provide specific actionable feedback

**Practical examples:**
- Post learning goals visibly and reference them during the lesson
- Provide tiered tasks at different difficulty levels (same concept, different complexity)
- Use flexible grouping — sometimes by interest, sometimes by readiness
- Give feedback that identifies what's working AND what to try next

### Guideline 3: Self-Regulation
Provide options for developing internal motivation and coping skills.

**Checkpoints:**
- **Promote expectations and beliefs that optimize motivation** — Teach growth mindset, model self-talk
- **Facilitate personal coping skills and strategies** — Teach self-regulation strategies explicitly
- **Develop self-assessment and reflection** — Build metacognitive skills

**Practical examples:**
- Teach students to set personal learning goals and track progress
- Provide self-monitoring checklists
- Model "thinking aloud" about your own learning struggles
- Include regular reflection prompts: "What was challenging? How did you handle it?"

## Principle 2: Multiple Means of Representation (The WHAT)

Learners differ in how they perceive and comprehend information.

### Guideline 4: Perception
Provide options for how information is presented to the senses.

**Checkpoints:**
- **Offer ways of customizing the display of information** — Adjustable font size, contrast, layout
- **Offer alternatives for auditory information** — Captions, transcripts, visual representations
- **Offer alternatives for visual information** — Text descriptions, audio descriptions, tactile options

**Practical examples:**
- Provide content in multiple formats: text + audio + video + diagram
- Use captions on all videos
- Ensure sufficient contrast and readable fonts
- Offer text-to-speech options

### Guideline 5: Language and Symbols
Provide options for understanding language, notation, and symbols.

**Checkpoints:**
- **Clarify vocabulary and symbols** — Pre-teach key terms, provide glossaries
- **Clarify syntax and structure** — Make structural relationships explicit
- **Support decoding of text, notation, and symbols** — Provide alternative representations
- **Promote understanding across languages** — Use multilingual supports
- **Illustrate through multiple media** — Pair text with images, diagrams, models

**Practical examples:**
- Create a visual word wall with images and definitions
- Provide graphic organizers that show how concepts relate
- Use analogies and metaphors to connect new ideas to familiar ones
- Allow students to access content in their home language

### Guideline 6: Comprehension
Provide options for building understanding and transferring knowledge.

**Checkpoints:**
- **Activate or supply background knowledge** — Pre-assessments, KWL charts, advance organizers
- **Highlight patterns, critical features, and big ideas** — Use visual emphasis, outlines, concept maps
- **Guide information processing and visualization** — Chunking, step-by-step guides, worked examples
- **Maximize transfer and generalization** — Explicitly teach for transfer, provide varied contexts

**Practical examples:**
- Begin lessons with a brief review of prior knowledge
- Use color-coding to highlight key concepts in text
- Provide worked examples before independent practice
- Ask: "Where else could you use this?"

## Principle 3: Multiple Means of Action and Expression (The HOW)

Learners differ in how they navigate learning and demonstrate what they know.

### Guideline 7: Physical Action
Provide options for interacting with materials and tools.

**Checkpoints:**
- **Vary the methods for response and navigation** — Keyboard, voice, touch, gestures
- **Optimize access to tools and assistive technologies** — Ensure tools are available and learners know how to use them

**Practical examples:**
- Allow responses via typing, speaking, drawing, or pointing
- Provide manipulatives alongside digital tools
- Ensure assistive technology is available and normalized

### Guideline 8: Expression and Communication
Provide options for how learners express what they know.

**Checkpoints:**
- **Use multiple media for communication** — Writing, speaking, drawing, video, building
- **Use multiple tools for construction and composition** — Word processors, graphic tools, recording devices
- **Build fluencies with graduated levels of support** — Scaffolds, templates, models, mentors

**Practical examples:**
- Choice of product: essay OR presentation OR podcast OR infographic OR model
- Provide templates and sentence starters for those who need them
- Allow use of speech-to-text, graphic organizers, or multimedia tools
- Scaffold complex tasks with step-by-step checklists

### Guideline 9: Executive Functions
Provide options for planning, managing, and monitoring learning.

**Checkpoints:**
- **Guide appropriate goal-setting** — Help learners set realistic, measurable goals
- **Support planning and strategy development** — Teach and model planning strategies
- **Facilitate managing information and resources** — Provide organizational tools
- **Enhance capacity for monitoring progress** — Rubrics, checklists, exemplars

**Practical examples:**
- Co-create project timelines with students
- Provide planning templates (goal, steps, resources, timeline)
- Teach note-taking and organization strategies explicitly
- Use rubrics shared BEFORE the task so students can self-monitor

## Barrier Removal vs. Retrofit

### The Retrofit Approach (Traditional)
1. Design curriculum for the "average" student
2. Teach the curriculum
3. Identify students who struggle
4. Create individual accommodations (IEPs, 504 plans)
5. Problem: Reactive, stigmatizing, resource-intensive, and benefits only identified students

### The UDL Approach (Proactive)
1. Anticipate variability from the start
2. Design flexible options into the curriculum
3. All students benefit from options
4. Fewer individual accommodations needed
5. Students who still need specific supports receive them without stigma

**The curb-cut analogy:**
- Curb cuts were designed for wheelchair users
- But they benefit everyone: parents with strollers, travelers with luggage, cyclists, delivery workers
- UDL works the same way: options designed for some benefit all

**Key shift:** The problem is not in the learner — the problem is in the curriculum. A rigid curriculum DISABLES learners. A flexible curriculum ENABLES them.

## UDL in Practice: Classroom Examples

### Example 1: Reading a Text (History)

**Without UDL:** All students read the same textbook chapter silently and answer comprehension questions.

**With UDL:**
- **Representation:** Text available in print, digital (with text-to-speech), audio recording, and simplified version; key vocabulary pre-taught with visual supports; graphic organizer provided
- **Engagement:** Students choose which historical figure to focus on; connect to current events; work individually or in pairs
- **Action/Expression:** Respond via written summary, recorded discussion, illustrated timeline, or digital presentation

### Example 2: Math Problem-Solving

**Without UDL:** Worksheet with 20 identical problems. Show your work in writing.

**With UDL:**
- **Representation:** Problems presented with diagrams AND numbers AND word form; manipulatives available; worked example posted
- **Engagement:** Students choose 10 of 15 problems; some problems use real-world contexts (shopping, cooking, building); self-paced
- **Action/Expression:** Show thinking via written work, video explanation, manipulative demonstration, or peer teaching

### Example 3: Science Lab

**Without UDL:** All students follow the same step-by-step procedure and write a lab report.

**With UDL:**
- **Representation:** Procedure available as text, video, and pictorial sequence; key safety terms highlighted
- **Engagement:** Students generate their own hypothesis before the lab; connect to real-world applications; choice of lab partners
- **Action/Expression:** Report via written lab report, video documentation, annotated photo essay, or oral presentation with visual support

## UDL Implementation Checklist

When designing a lesson or unit, check:

**Engagement:**
- [ ] Is there choice in how students engage? (topic, tools, grouping, pace)
- [ ] Is the content connected to students' lives and interests?
- [ ] Is the learning environment psychologically safe?
- [ ] Are goals clear and visible?
- [ ] Is feedback growth-oriented and specific?

**Representation:**
- [ ] Is content available in more than one format? (text + audio + visual)
- [ ] Are key vocabulary and symbols pre-taught or supported?
- [ ] Is background knowledge activated?
- [ ] Are patterns and big ideas highlighted?

**Action and Expression:**
- [ ] Can students demonstrate learning in more than one way?
- [ ] Are tools and technology accessible?
- [ ] Are scaffolds available (and removable)?
- [ ] Do students have support for planning and self-monitoring?

## Anti-patterns

1. **Designing for the average** — There is no average learner; variability is the norm
2. **Retrofitting only** — Adding accommodations after the fact instead of designing flexibly from the start
3. **Options = lower standards** — UDL provides multiple PATHS to the same high standards, not lower expectations
4. **UDL as a checklist** — UDL is a mindset and framework, not a compliance exercise
5. **Technology = UDL** — Technology can support UDL but is not required; low-tech options are equally valid
6. **One accommodation fits all** — Different learners need different options, not one alternative
7. **Removing all challenge** — UDL provides support, not an easy path; productive struggle is preserved
8. **Ignoring student input** — Students are the best source of information about what helps them learn
9. **UDL only for "special needs"** — UDL benefits ALL learners, not just those with identified disabilities
10. **Perfection paralysis** — You don't need to implement all guidelines at once; start with one principle and iterate
