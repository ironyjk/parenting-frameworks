# Triple P Positive Parenting Program Skill

> "Good parenting is not about being perfect. It's about being good enough — and knowing what to do when things go wrong." — Matthew Sanders

## Overview

Triple P (Positive Parenting Program) was developed by Professor Matthew Sanders at the University of Queensland, Australia. It is one of the most extensively researched parenting programs in the world, with over 600 published studies across 30+ countries demonstrating its effectiveness.

Triple P is a **multi-level, public health approach** to parenting support. Rather than a single method, it provides a graduated system of interventions — from light-touch universal information to intensive family therapy — matched to the level of need. Its core framework emphasizes **self-regulation** for both parents and children.

**Note:** Triple P is a registered program name. This skill describes the evidence-based framework and principles for educational reference, not as a substitute for certified Triple P practitioner guidance.

## The Five Levels

### Level 1: Universal (Information)

**Target:** All parents in the community
**Format:** Media campaigns, tip sheets, brief information
**Content:** General positive parenting messages, normalizing challenges
**Example:** Posters in pediatric offices, parenting websites, brief videos

### Level 2: Selected (Brief Intervention)

**Target:** Parents with specific concerns about child development or behavior
**Format:** 1-2 session seminars, brief telephone consultations
**Content:** Anticipatory guidance for specific developmental issues (toilet training, sleep, sibling rivalry)
**Example:** A parent asks the pediatrician about tantrums; receives a targeted strategy sheet and brief coaching

### Level 3: Primary Care (Narrow Focus)

**Target:** Parents with a specific behavioral concern requiring active skills training
**Format:** 3-4 sessions with a practitioner, focused on one or two target behaviors
**Content:** Active skills training for specific problems, with practice and review
**Example:** A child consistently refuses to go to bed; parent receives structured support on bedtime routine management

### Level 4: Standard/Group (Broad Focus)

**Target:** Parents dealing with multiple behavioral concerns or a broader pattern
**Format:** 8-10 sessions (individual or group), comprehensive skills training
**Content:** Full positive parenting skills program — observation, encouragement, managing misbehavior, planning ahead, self-regulation
**Example:** Parent reports child has aggression, non-compliance, and sibling conflict; enters a group program covering the full Triple P toolkit

### Level 5: Enhanced (Intensive Family Intervention)

**Target:** Families with severe behavioral issues PLUS family dysfunction (parental depression, marital conflict, high stress)
**Format:** Level 4 + additional modules for partner support, mood management, stress coping
**Content:** Addresses parenting skills AND the family context that maintains problems
**Example:** Child with severe conduct problems in a family experiencing domestic stress, parental mental health challenges, and social isolation

## Five Core Positive Parenting Principles

### 1. Ensure a Safe and Engaging Environment

Children need a safe environment that provides opportunities for exploration, learning, and play.

**Strategies:**
- Childproof the environment to reduce the need for constant "no"
- Provide age-appropriate activities and materials
- Supervise actively without hovering
- Create routines that provide predictability

**Dialogue example:**
```
Parent: [Before a long car trip, packs activity bag]
         "We're going on a long drive. I put some new coloring pages and 
         your favorite audiobook in your backpack. Want to pick one more 
         thing to bring?"
Child:  "My dinosaurs!"
Parent: "Good choice. That should make the ride go faster."
```

### 2. Create a Positive Learning Environment

Use everyday interactions as teaching moments. Children learn best through positive engagement.

**Strategies:**
- **Incidental teaching** — when the child shows interest, expand on it
- **Ask-Say-Do** — ask the child to try, show them how, practice together
- **Model behavior** — children do what they SEE, not what they're TOLD

**Dialogue example (incidental teaching):**
```
Child:  [pointing at a ladybug] "Bug!"
Parent: "Yes! That's a ladybug. See the spots? Can you count them?"
Child:  "One, two, three... four!"
Parent: "Four spots! Ladybugs are helpful — they eat tiny bugs that 
         hurt plants. Shall we watch what it does next?"
```

### 3. Use Assertive Discipline

Not aggressive. Not passive. Assertive: clear, calm, consistent.

**Strategies (graduated response):**
1. **Descriptive praise** — specific feedback on desired behavior
2. **Setting clear ground rules** — few, fair, enforceable
3. **Directed discussion** — brief explanation of why a rule exists
4. **Planned ignoring** — strategically withdrawing attention from minor misbehavior
5. **Clear, calm instructions** — state what to do, not what NOT to do
6. **Logical consequences** — related, reasonable, respectful
7. **Quiet time** — brief removal from the reinforcing situation (not the room)
8. **Time-out** — last resort, brief, calm, for serious misbehavior only

### 4. Have Realistic Expectations

Mismatch between expectations and developmental capacity creates conflict.

**Key principles:**
- Understand what is normal for the child's age
- Avoid perfectionism — aim for "good enough" behavior
- Recognize that learning takes repetition (not just one instruction)
- Accept that ALL children misbehave — it is part of development
- Set goals for yourself as a parent that are achievable

### 5. Take Care of Yourself as a Parent

Parenting quality depends on parent wellbeing. Exhausted, stressed, depressed parents cannot sustain positive strategies.

**Strategies:**
- Identify personal stress triggers
- Develop coping strategies (exercise, social support, respite)
- Address relationship issues that affect parenting
- Challenge unhelpful thinking patterns ("I'm a terrible parent")
- Ask for help — this is strength, not weakness

## Key Strategies in Detail

### Descriptive Praise

The most powerful tool in the Triple P toolkit. Specific, immediate feedback on behavior you want to see more of.

**Formula:** Describe exactly what the child did + express approval

| Generic praise | Descriptive praise |
|---|---|
| "Good job!" | "You put all the blocks back in the box without being asked. That's really helpful." |
| "Well done." | "You waited patiently while I was on the phone. That took a lot of self-control." |
| "Good boy." | "You used your words to tell your sister how you felt instead of hitting. I noticed that." |

**Dialogue example:**
```
Parent: [child shares a toy with sibling without being asked]
         "I just saw you give your brother a turn with the truck. 
         That was really generous. Look how happy he is!"
Child:  [smiles, continues sharing]
```

**Rules:**
- Be immediate (within seconds of the behavior)
- Be specific (not vague approval)
- Be genuine (don't over-praise trivial things)
- Aim for 6:1 ratio — six positive interactions for every one correction

### Planned Activities

Prevent misbehavior by planning ahead for high-risk situations.

**Structure:**
1. **Identify the high-risk situation** (shopping, restaurants, visitors)
2. **Plan ahead** — discuss rules, prepare activities, set expectations
3. **Brief the child** — tell them what will happen and what you expect
4. **Engage the child** — give them a role during the activity
5. **Debrief** — praise what went well afterward

**Dialogue example (before grocery shopping):**
```
Parent: "We're going to the store. Remember our three rules?"
Child:  "Stay close, hands to myself, use inside voice."
Parent: "Right. And what's your job?"
Child:  "Find the apples and the bread!"
Parent: "Exactly. If things go well, we'll have time to stop at the 
         park on the way home."
```

### Behavior Charts

For specific, targeted behaviors that need consistent reinforcement.

**Design principles:**
- Maximum 3 behaviors at a time
- Behaviors must be specific and observable
- Child participates in creating the chart
- Reward is pre-agreed, reasonable, and timely
- Phase out once behavior is established (fade the chart)

**Dialogue example (setting up):**
```
Parent: "Let's make a chart for three things we're working on. What are 
         our morning goals?"
Child:  "Get dressed, brush teeth, put shoes on."
Parent: "Right. Each morning you do all three before the timer goes off, 
         you get a star. Five stars and we'll [agreed reward]."
Child:  "Can I decorate the chart?"
Parent: "Absolutely — it's YOUR chart."
```

### Quiet Time and Time-Out

**Quiet time:** The child remains in the situation but is briefly removed from positive attention (e.g., sits at the edge of play area for 30 seconds to 2 minutes).

**Time-out:** The child is briefly removed to a boring (not frightening) location. Used ONLY for pre-specified serious behaviors (hitting, biting, deliberate destruction).

**Rules for time-out:**
- Maximum duration: 1 minute per year of age (5-year-old = 5 minutes max)
- Calm voice: "You hit your sister. Time-out." (no lecture, no anger)
- Timer starts when the child is calm
- Afterward: brief reconnection, no lengthy discussion
- NEVER use a dark, scary, or locked space

**Dialogue example:**
```
Parent: [Child hits sibling]
         [Calm voice] "You hit your brother. That's time-out."
         [Guides child to time-out spot]
         "When the timer goes, you can come back and play."
         [After time-out ends]
         "Time-out is over. Are you ready to play without hitting?"
Child:  "Yes."
Parent: "Good. Let's go."
         [No lecture. No guilt. Clean slate.]
```

## The Self-Regulation Framework

Triple P's deepest layer: teaching parents to become their own behavior change agents.

### For Parents

1. **Self-sufficiency** — develop your own problem-solving skills, not dependence on experts
2. **Self-efficacy** — believe in your ability to manage parenting challenges
3. **Self-management** — set personal goals, monitor your own behavior, reward yourself
4. **Personal agency** — recognize that you can influence outcomes through your choices

### For Children

The ultimate goal: children who can regulate their own behavior, emotions, and attention without external control.

**Building self-regulation:**
- Give choices (autonomy builds self-regulation)
- Gradually reduce external scaffolding as skills build
- Teach self-monitoring: "How do you think that went?"
- Praise self-correction: "You started to grab it, then stopped and asked. That's self-control."

## Anti-patterns

1. **Jumping to time-out** — it is the last tool, not the first. Exhaust positive strategies first.
2. **Vague praise** — "Good job" teaches nothing. Be specific.
3. **Inconsistency** — applying rules sometimes but not others. Children learn from patterns.
4. **Emotional time-out** — using time-out while angry turns it into punishment, not teaching.
5. **Too many rules** — 3-5 clear rules are better than 20 vague ones.
6. **Ignoring parent self-care** — burned-out parents cannot sustain positive strategies.
7. **Expecting strategies to work immediately** — behavior change takes 2-4 weeks of consistency.
8. **Using behavior charts forever** — they are training wheels, not permanent fixtures. Fade them.
9. **Skipping planned activities** — prevention is easier than intervention.
