# Formative Assessment Skill

> "When the cook tastes the soup, that's formative assessment. When the customer tastes the soup, that's summative assessment." — Paul Black

## Overview

Formative assessment is the process of gathering evidence about student learning DURING instruction to adjust teaching and improve outcomes. Unlike summative assessment (tests, finals, grades), formative assessment is not about measuring and ranking — it is about informing the next instructional move. Research by Black and Wiliam (1998) showed that effective formative assessment produces learning gains equivalent to moving an "average" student into the top 35% — one of the largest effect sizes in educational research.

---

## Five Key Strategies (Wiliam, 2011)

Dylan Wiliam organized formative assessment into five strategies across three processes and three roles:

|  | Where the learner is going | Where the learner is right now | How to get there |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Teacher** | 1. Clarify learning intentions and success criteria | 2. Engineer effective discussions, activities, and tasks that elicit evidence of learning | 3. Provide feedback that moves learners forward |
| **Peer** | Understand learning intentions and success criteria | 4. Activate students as instructional resources for one another | |
| **Learner** | Understand learning intentions and success criteria | | 5. Activate students as owners of their own learning |

---

### Strategy 1: Clarify Learning Intentions and Success Criteria

Students cannot assess their own progress if they don't know where they're going.

**Learning intentions** answer: "What will you learn today?" (not "What will you do today?")
- Activity-focused (AVOID): "Today we will read Chapter 5 and answer questions"
- Learning-focused (USE): "Today you will learn how photosynthesis converts sunlight into chemical energy"

**Success criteria** answer: "How will you know you've learned it?"
- Specific and observable: "I can explain the three stages of photosynthesis and describe where each occurs"
- Shared with students BEFORE the lesson (not revealed after)
- Co-constructed with students when appropriate

**Techniques:**
- Share learning intentions at the start AND return to them at the end
- Post success criteria visibly; students check off as they demonstrate each
- Use exemplars and rubrics so students can see what "good" looks like
- Have students restate the learning intention in their own words

### Strategy 2: Engineer Effective Discussion and Tasks That Elicit Evidence

Design activities that make student thinking VISIBLE to the teacher. The teacher cannot adjust instruction based on evidence they don't have.

**The fundamental principle:** Every question, task, and activity should be designed to generate information about what students understand (and misunderstand).

**Techniques:**

**Exit Tickets (most versatile technique)**
- 2-3 minute written response at the end of class
- One question targeting the day's learning intention
- Teacher reviews ALL tickets before next lesson
- Sorts into: got it / partially / not yet → plans next lesson accordingly
- Example: "Explain in 2 sentences why the water cycle matters for agriculture"

**Hinge Questions**
- A carefully designed multiple-choice question given at a critical "hinge point" in the lesson
- Every wrong answer reveals a SPECIFIC misconception
- The teacher can diagnose what went wrong, not just that something went wrong
- Example (fractions): "Which is larger: 3/7 or 3/5?"
  - Answer A: 3/7 (misconception: larger denominator = larger fraction)
  - Answer B: 3/5 (correct)
  - Answer C: they're equal (misconception: same numerator = equal)

**Think-Pair-Share**
- Think: Individual reflection (30-60 seconds of silence — enforce the silence)
- Pair: Discuss with a partner (1-2 minutes)
- Share: Selected pairs share with class
- Enhancement: After sharing, ask a different pair to build on or challenge what was said

**Mini Whiteboards**
- Every student holds up their answer simultaneously
- Teacher sees ALL responses instantly (not just the hand-raisers)
- No hiding — every student must commit to an answer
- Eliminates the illusion that the class "gets it" because 3 eager students answered

**Traffic Lights (Red/Yellow/Green)**
- Students self-assess their understanding using colored cards, cups, or signals
- Green: "I understand and can explain it to someone else"
- Yellow: "I think I understand but I'm not sure"
- Red: "I'm confused and need help"
- Teacher scans the room and adjusts accordingly
- Caution: Students may not self-assess accurately — combine with other techniques

**Random Selection (No Opt-Out)**
- Use popsicle sticks, random name generators, or numbered cards to call on students
- EVERY student knows they might be called, so EVERY student must think
- If a student says "I don't know," do NOT move on. Provide a scaffolded question, let them listen to a peer, then return to them.
- This is not about catching students off guard — it's about creating universal engagement

**ABCD Cards**
- Students hold up a card (A, B, C, or D) to answer multiple-choice questions
- All students answer simultaneously
- Teacher can see the distribution instantly
- More engaging than digital polling for many contexts

### Strategy 3: Provide Feedback That Moves Learners Forward

Not all feedback is equal. Research shows that feedback can have zero or even NEGATIVE effects if done poorly.

**Effective feedback is:**
- **Specific** — "Your thesis statement is clear, but your second paragraph doesn't provide evidence to support it" (not "Good job" or "Needs work")
- **Actionable** — Tells the student what to DO next, not just what's wrong
- **Timely** — Soon enough to be relevant (minutes to days, not weeks)
- **Focused on the task, not the person** — "This paragraph needs more evidence" not "You're a weak writer"
- **Forward-looking** — "Next time, try..." or "Here's how to improve this..."

**Feedback that does NOT work:**
- **Grades alone** — A letter or number tells students WHERE they are but not HOW to improve
- **Grades + comments** — Research shows students ignore comments when a grade is present (Butler, 1988)
- **Praise without specifics** — "Great work!" is motivating but not informative
- **Too much feedback** — Overwhelms; limit to 1-3 actionable points

**The Comment-Only Marking approach:**
Instead of grading student work, provide only written comments. Students read and respond to the comments. Grade comes later (if needed). This forces engagement with the feedback.

**Feedback structures:**
| Structure | Format |
|-----------|--------|
| **Stars and Stairs** | What's working well (stars) + What to improve (stairs) |
| **Two Stars and a Wish** | Two specific strengths + one specific improvement |
| **Medal and Mission** | What you've achieved (medal) + Your next challenge (mission) |
| **Glow and Grow** | Where you shine (glow) + Where to develop (grow) |

### Strategy 4: Activate Students as Instructional Resources for One Another

Peer assessment is not about students grading each other. It is about students learning to evaluate work against criteria — a skill that directly builds self-assessment capacity.

**Techniques:**

**Two Stars and a Wish (Peer Version)**
- Partner identifies two specific strengths and one specific suggestion
- Must reference success criteria
- The writer then revises based on feedback

**Gallery Walk with Feedback**
- Work posted around the room
- Students circulate and leave specific feedback on sticky notes
- Feedback must reference the success criteria

**Peer Teaching**
- Students who understand explain to those who don't
- The explainer benefits as much as the listener (explaining deepens understanding)
- Structured: "You have 3 minutes. Explain [concept] to your partner using the key words on the board."

**C3B4ME (See Three Before Me)**
- Before asking the teacher for help, consult three classmates first
- Promotes independence and peer support
- Teacher is not the only source of expertise

**Ground rules for peer assessment:**
1. Always reference the success criteria (not personal opinion)
2. Be specific (not "I like it" but "Your opening hook connects to the reader because...")
3. Be kind and constructive
4. The person receiving feedback says "Thank you" and considers (does not argue)

### Strategy 5: Activate Students as Owners of Their Own Learning

The ultimate goal: students who can assess their own work, identify gaps, and take action without the teacher.

**Techniques:**

**Self-Assessment Against Criteria**
- Students evaluate their own work using the same rubric the teacher uses
- Compare self-assessment to teacher assessment — discuss discrepancies
- Builds calibration over time

**Traffic Light Self-Rating**
- After each learning intention, students rate their confidence
- Teacher uses the data; students use it to direct their study

**Learning Journals/Logs**
- Regular entries: "What did I learn? What am I confused about? What will I do about it?"
- Not a diary — structured metacognitive reflection

**Goal Setting and Tracking**
- Students set specific, measurable learning goals
- Track progress weekly
- Reflect: "What strategy is working? What should I change?"

**KWL (Know-Want to Know-Learned)**
- Before: What do I already Know? What do I Want to know?
- After: What did I Learn?
- Makes learning conscious and directed

## The Formative Assessment Cycle

```
1. CLARIFY what students should learn (learning intention + success criteria)
       ↓
2. ELICIT evidence of current understanding (techniques from Strategy 2)
       ↓
3. INTERPRET the evidence (What do students understand? What are the misconceptions?)
       ↓
4. ACT on the evidence:
   - Teacher adjusts instruction (re-teach, extend, differentiate)
   - Students adjust learning (seek help, practice more, try new strategy)
       ↓
5. Return to Step 2 → continuous cycle
```

**The cycle must be rapid.** If you collect evidence on Monday and adjust on Friday, you've lost four days. The best formative assessment happens within a lesson: teach → check → adjust → check again.

## Common Assessment Techniques Quick Reference

| Technique | Time | Evidence Type | Best For |
|-----------|------|---------------|----------|
| Exit ticket | 2-3 min | Written | End-of-lesson check |
| Hinge question | 1-2 min | Multiple choice | Mid-lesson decision point |
| Mini whiteboard | 1 min | Written/drawn | Quick whole-class check |
| Think-pair-share | 3-5 min | Verbal | Activating all learners |
| Traffic lights | 30 sec | Self-report | Quick confidence gauge |
| Random calling | Ongoing | Verbal | Universal participation |
| Gallery walk | 10-15 min | Written/visual | Peer feedback on drafts |
| One-minute paper | 1-2 min | Written | "What's the muddiest point?" |
| Fist to five | 10 sec | Gestural | Quick agreement/understanding check |
| Concept map | 5-10 min | Visual/written | Checking connections between ideas |

## Anti-patterns

1. **Assessment OF learning masquerading as formative** — Graded quizzes that count toward the final grade are not formative, even if called "formative"
2. **Collecting evidence but not acting on it** — Exit tickets filed away without informing the next lesson
3. **Only checking the eager hand-raisers** — 3 students answer; teacher assumes the class understands
4. **Feedback as grades** — Numbers and letters without actionable guidance
5. **Delayed feedback** — Returning work 3 weeks later (the learning moment has passed)
6. **Feedback overload** — 15 comments on a paper that the student will never read
7. **Student self-assessment without criteria** — "How do you think you did?" without specific standards to evaluate against
8. **Punishing wrong answers** — If students fear being wrong, they will hide their misunderstandings
9. **One technique for everything** — Using only exit tickets (or only any single technique)
10. **Skipping Strategy 1** — Without clear learning intentions, there is nothing to assess against
