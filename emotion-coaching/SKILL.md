# Emotion Coaching Skill

> "In the last decade or so, science has discovered a tremendous amount about the role emotions play in our lives. Researchers have found that even more than IQ, your emotional awareness and ability to handle feelings will determine your success and happiness in all walks of life." — John Gottman

## Overview

Emotion Coaching is a parenting approach developed by Dr. John Gottman based on decades of research at the Gottman Institute. Through observing parent-child interactions and tracking long-term outcomes, Gottman found that children whose parents practiced Emotion Coaching had better physical health, higher academic achievement, stronger friendships, fewer behavioral problems, and greater emotional resilience.

The core insight: **emotions are not the problem; behavior might be.** All feelings are acceptable. Not all behaviors are. The parent's job is to be a guide through the child's emotional landscape — not to dismiss, suppress, or fix the feelings.

## Four Parenting Meta-Emotion Styles

How parents feel about their own and their children's emotions determines their style:

### 1. Dismissing Parent

**Belief:** Negative emotions are harmful, unimportant, or a waste of time.

**Behaviors:**
- Ignores or trivializes the child's feelings
- Wants negative emotions to go away quickly
- May distract the child from feelings
- "It's not a big deal" / "You're fine" / "Just cheer up"

**Impact on child:** Learns emotions are wrong. Difficulty regulating emotions. Feels something is wrong with them for having feelings.

**Example:**
```
Child:  "I'm scared of the dark."
Parent: "There's nothing to be scared of. You're a big kid now. Go to sleep."
```

### 2. Disapproving Parent

**Belief:** Negative emotions are a sign of weakness or bad character that must be corrected.

**Behaviors:**
- Criticizes the child for having emotions
- Punishes emotional expression
- "Stop crying or I'll give you something to cry about"
- Views emotions as something to be controlled/suppressed

**Impact on child:** Shame about emotions. May become aggressive (learned suppression explodes) or shut down emotionally.

**Example:**
```
Child:  [crying after losing a game]
Parent: "Stop being such a sore loser. Nobody likes a crybaby."
```

### 3. Laissez-Faire Parent

**Belief:** All emotions should be freely expressed and accepted — including all behaviors.

**Behaviors:**
- Accepts and empathizes with emotions (good!)
- BUT fails to set limits on behavior or guide problem-solving
- Offers no guidance on what to DO with feelings
- "I understand you're angry" [while child destroys toys]

**Impact on child:** Difficulty self-regulating. Doesn't learn how to solve problems. May struggle socially because no one else tolerates unlimited emotional expression.

**Example:**
```
Child:  [screaming and throwing blocks because tower fell]
Parent: "I can see you're really frustrated, honey."
         [does nothing else while blocks fly across the room]
```

### 4. Emotion Coaching Parent

**Belief:** Negative emotions are an opportunity for intimacy and teaching. All feelings are valid; not all behaviors are okay.

**Behaviors:**
- Aware of the child's emotions, even subtle ones
- Sees negative emotions as an opportunity to connect and teach
- Validates and labels emotions
- THEN sets limits and helps problem-solve

**Impact on child:** Trusts their own feelings. Can self-soothe. Better at problem-solving. Stronger relationships. Higher academic performance. Better physical health.

## The Five Steps of Emotion Coaching

### Step 1: Be Aware of the Child's Emotion

Notice emotions BEFORE they escalate. Tune in to:
- Facial expressions, body language, tone of voice
- Context (what just happened?)
- Lower-intensity emotions (irritation before rage, worry before panic)
- Your OWN emotional reaction (your body often mirrors theirs)

**The key:** Emotion Coaching works BEST at lower intensities. Catch the frustration before it becomes a meltdown. Catch the worry before it becomes a refusal.

### Step 2: Recognize the Emotion as an Opportunity for Connection and Teaching

This is the mindset shift.

| Without Step 2 | With Step 2 |
|---|---|
| "Oh no, she's crying again" | "She's telling me something important" |
| "Here we go with another tantrum" | "This is a chance to help him learn" |
| "I need to stop this behavior" | "I need to understand what's underneath" |

**Reframe:** The meltdown in the grocery store is not a public embarrassment — it is a child who needs help with an overwhelming emotion.

### Step 3: Listen Empathically and Validate

Give full attention. Reflect what you hear. Name what you see.

**Techniques:**
- Get on the child's physical level (crouch, sit on the floor)
- "Tell me about it" / "What happened?"
- Reflect: "So you felt [X] when [Y] happened"
- Validate: "That makes sense" / "I can understand why you'd feel that way"
- Do NOT: advise, fix, minimize, lecture, or redirect (yet)

**Example dialogue:**
```
Child:  [slams backpack, sits on floor, arms crossed]
Parent: [sits down next to child, waits a moment]
Parent: "Rough day?"
Child:  "I HATE art class."
Parent: "Something happened in art class that really upset you."
Child:  "Mrs. Kim said my painting wasn't finished and I have to stay 
         in at recess to work on it."
Parent: "Oh. So you're going to miss recess, and that feels unfair."
Child:  "I WAS finished! She just didn't like it!"
Parent: "You felt like you'd done your best work and she didn't see that. 
         That's really frustrating — to put effort in and feel like it 
         wasn't recognized."
Child:  [softens slightly] "Yeah."
```

### Step 4: Help the Child Name the Emotion

Children often feel emotions as a confusing physical storm. Naming the emotion activates the prefrontal cortex and begins to regulate the limbic system. Research shows that labeling emotions literally calms the brain.

**Language by age:**

| Age Range | Emotion Vocabulary | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 2-3 years | Basic 4: happy, sad, mad, scared | "You look mad. You wanted that toy." |
| 4-5 years | Add: frustrated, worried, excited, lonely, jealous, embarrassed | "It sounds like you're frustrated because you can't make it work." |
| 6-8 years | Add: disappointed, anxious, overwhelmed, left out, proud, confused, guilty | "Are you feeling disappointed because you expected to win?" |
| 9-12 years | Add: humiliated, betrayed, insecure, conflicted, resentful, nostalgic | "It sounds like you feel betrayed — like a trust was broken." |
| 13+ years | Full adult vocabulary + nuance, ambivalence, mixed emotions | "You seem both relieved and sad about this — like two feelings at once." |

**How to name:**
- Offer, don't impose: "Could it be that you're feeling [X]?" is better than "You're angry."
- Accept corrections: "Are you sad?" "No, I'm MAD." "Ah, you're mad. Thanks for telling me."
- Name mixed emotions: "It sounds like you're excited about the trip AND nervous about being away from home. Both feelings can be true at the same time."

### Step 5: Set Limits and Help Problem-Solve

AFTER (not instead of) the first four steps, now address behavior and solutions.

**The structure:**
1. Validate the feeling
2. Set the limit on behavior
3. Help identify what they COULD do instead

**Example dialogue:**
```
Parent: "I can see you're really angry at your brother. You have every 
         right to be angry — he broke your model. Being angry is okay. 
         AND hitting is not okay, no matter how angry you are."
Child:  "But he BROKE it!"
Parent: "He did. And that's worth being furious about. When you're this 
         angry, what could you do INSTEAD of hitting?"
Child:  "I don't know."
Parent: "Some kids stomp their feet, or squeeze a pillow, or come tell 
         a grown-up. Which of those might work for you?"
Child:  "Tell you."
Parent: "Good. And then we'll figure out what to do about the broken 
         model together. Let's go talk to your brother."
```

## Full Example: Emotion Coaching a Tantrum (Age 5)

Context: Child wanted a toy at the store. Parent said no. Child is screaming.

```
[Step 1: Awareness — parent notices rising distress before the scream]

[Step 2: Opportunity — "This is hard for her. She needs help, not 
punishment."]

[Step 3: Empathic Listening]
Parent: [crouches to child's level, speaks quietly]
         "You really, really wanted that toy."
Child:  "I WANT IT! YOU NEVER BUY ME ANYTHING!"
Parent: "It feels like I say no a lot. And right now you wanted this 
         one so badly."

[Step 4: Name the Emotion]
Parent: "You're disappointed. And maybe a little angry at me for 
         saying no."
Child:  [still crying but volume decreases] "Yes..."
Parent: "Disappointment is a hard feeling. It's okay to be disappointed."

[Step 5: Limits + Problem-Solve]
Parent: "It's okay to feel disappointed. AND screaming in the store 
         isn't something we do. Would you like to put it on your 
         birthday list? Or draw a picture of it when we get home?"
Child:  [sniffling] "Birthday list."
Parent: "Let's write it down right now so we don't forget."
```

## When NOT to Emotion Coach

Emotion Coaching is powerful but not always the right tool:

1. **Immediate safety threat** — A child running toward the street needs "STOP!" not "I can see you're excited to chase that ball."

2. **You are flooded** — If your own emotions are at a 7+ out of 10, you cannot coach. Stabilize yourself first: "I need a minute to calm down, and then we'll talk."

3. **The child is manipulating through emotion** — A child who has learned to cry on demand to get candy. The test: is this a genuine emotion or a strategy? If strategic, calmly hold the limit without the coaching steps.

4. **Time pressure / wrong setting** — "We need to leave for school in 2 minutes" is not the time for deep emotion coaching. Acknowledge briefly: "I can see you're upset. We need to go now, AND we will talk about this in the car."

5. **The child needs space** — Some children (especially teens) need to process alone before talking. "I can see you're upset. I'm here when you're ready to talk."

6. **Repeated low-stakes situations** — If a child is mildly annoyed about something trivial, full 5-step coaching is overkill. A simple acknowledgment suffices: "Yeah, that's annoying."

## Building Your Own Emotional Awareness

You cannot coach emotions you cannot recognize in yourself. Gottman's research found that parents who are aware of their OWN emotions are far better at coaching their children's.

**Daily practice:**
1. Three times a day, pause and ask: "What am I feeling right now?"
2. Name it specifically (not "fine" or "stressed" — be precise)
3. Notice where you feel it in your body
4. Notice what triggered it

**Your own emotional history matters.** If your parents were Dismissing or Disapproving, you may instinctively replicate that. Awareness is the first step to breaking the pattern.

---

## Anti-patterns

1. **Skipping to Step 5** — "I know you're upset BUT here's what you should do" bypasses the connection
2. **Emotion Coaching as a technique to stop crying** — the goal is connection and teaching, not silence
3. **Naming emotions incorrectly and insisting** — "You're sad." "I'm NOT sad!" "Yes you are."
4. **Coaching during a full meltdown** — the child's prefrontal cortex is offline. Wait for partial calm.
5. **No limits (Laissez-Faire trap)** — validating feelings without addressing problematic behavior
6. **Using it only for negative emotions** — coach positive emotions too! "You look so proud of that!"
7. **Coaching someone else's child uninvited** — model it with your own kids; let others observe
8. **Expecting immediate calm** — Emotion Coaching reduces intensity over TIME, not instantly
