# Collaborative & Proactive Solutions (CPS) Skill

> "Kids do well if they can. If they can't, something is getting in the way — and our job is to figure out what that is." — Ross Greene

## Overview

Collaborative & Proactive Solutions (CPS), developed by Dr. Ross Greene, is a radically different approach to understanding and addressing challenging behavior in children. Its foundational philosophy replaces "kids do well if they want to" with **"kids do well if they can."**

Challenging behavior is not the result of poor motivation, bad parenting, or willful defiance. It is the result of **lagging skills** — the child lacks the cognitive skills (flexibility, frustration tolerance, problem-solving) to meet certain expectations. The solution is not to motivate harder (rewards/punishments) but to **build skills and solve problems collaboratively**.

## The Philosophy Shift

### Two Lenses on Challenging Behavior

| Traditional Lens | CPS Lens |
|---|---|
| Kids do well if they **want** to | Kids do well if they **can** |
| Misbehavior = poor motivation | Challenging behavior = lagging skills + unsolved problems |
| Solution: motivate (reward/punish) | Solution: teach skills + solve problems collaboratively |
| The child is the problem | The mismatch between expectations and skills is the problem |
| "What consequence should I impose?" | "What skill is lacking? What problem needs solving?" |

**Key insight:** A child who can meet an expectation WILL meet it. If they're not meeting it, something — a lagging cognitive skill — is getting in the way. Challenging behavior occurs when demands outstrip a child's capacity to respond adaptively.

## ALSUP: Assessment of Lagging Skills and Unsolved Problems

The ALSUP is the starting point. It has two columns:

### Column 1: Lagging Skills

Identify which cognitive skills the child has difficulty with:

- **Flexibility / Adaptability** — difficulty handling transitions, adjusting to changes in plan
- **Frustration Tolerance** — low threshold for frustration, becomes overwhelmed easily
- **Problem-Solving** — difficulty thinking of multiple solutions, seeing options
- **Emotional Regulation** — difficulty managing emotional response to frustration
- **Social Thinking** — difficulty understanding others' perspectives, reading social cues
- **Language Processing** — difficulty expressing concerns, needs, or problems in words
- **Cognitive Flexibility** — black-and-white thinking, difficulty seeing gray areas
- **Executive Functioning** — difficulty with organization, planning, time management, shifting attention
- **Sensory/Emotional Sensitivity** — heightened sensitivity to sensory stimuli or emotions
- **Chronic Irritability** — persistent mood issues affecting flexibility

### Column 2: Unsolved Problems

List the specific, predictable situations where challenging behavior occurs. Unsolved problems are:

- **Specific** — not "has tantrums" but "difficulty getting ready for school on time"
- **Observable** — "has difficulty sharing toys with younger brother during free play"
- **Free of adult theories** — NOT "refuses to do homework because he's lazy"
- **Expectation-focused** — what the child is having difficulty DOING

**Good unsolved problems:**
- "Difficulty getting off the tablet when screen time is over"
- "Difficulty staying in his seat during dinner"
- "Difficulty completing homework without becoming upset"
- "Difficulty brushing teeth before bed"

**Bad unsolved problems (too vague, contains judgments):**
- "Won't listen" (vague)
- "Is defiant" (label)
- "Refuses to cooperate" (judgment about motivation)
- "Has anger issues" (too broad)

## The Three Plans

### Plan A: Impose Your Will

The adult imposes the solution unilaterally.

- "You ARE going to do your homework NOW."
- Result: Increases challenging behavior. The child already lacks the skills to meet this demand — forcing it guarantees a meltdown.
- **Use only for genuine safety emergencies** (running into traffic, etc.)

### Plan B: Collaborate (THE CORE OF CPS)

Solve the problem WITH the child using three steps.

### Plan C: Drop It (For Now)

Intentionally set aside an expectation — for now.

- NOT giving in. NOT permissive.
- Strategic prioritization. You can't work on every unsolved problem at once.
- Reduces the overall load of demands so the child (and family) can function
- "We're going to let the messy room go for now and focus on the morning routine"

**Choosing Plans:**
- List all unsolved problems from the ALSUP
- Prioritize 2-3 for Plan B
- Place the rest in Plan C
- Use Plan A only for immediate safety

## Plan B: The Three Steps

Plan B is done **proactively** (not in the heat of the moment). You already know the unsolved problems because they are PREDICTABLE.

### Step 1: Empathy Step — Gather Information

**Goal:** Understand the child's concern, perspective, or difficulty regarding the unsolved problem.

**How:**
- State the observation neutrally: "I've noticed you're having difficulty with [unsolved problem]."
- Use a genuine inquiry: "What's up?" / "What's going on with that?"
- **LISTEN. Do not solve yet.**
- Drill down with reflective listening and clarifying questions
- Keep going until you have a clear, specific understanding of the child's concern

**Example dialogue:**
```
Parent: "I've noticed that when it's time to turn off the tablet, things 
         get really hard. What's up with that?"
Child:  "I don't want to stop!"
Parent: "I hear you — you don't want to stop. Can you tell me more? 
         What makes it hard to stop?"
Child:  "I'm always in the middle of something."
Parent: "Ah, so you feel like you're being interrupted right in the 
         middle. Is there more?"
Child:  "Yeah, and I never know when it's coming. You just say 'time's up' 
         and I haven't saved my game."
Parent: "So it feels sudden, and you lose your progress. That IS 
         frustrating. Anything else?"
Child:  "No, that's it."
```

**Critical rules for the Empathy Step:**
- Do NOT skip it. Do NOT rush it. This is where most adults fail.
- Do NOT insert your concern yet (that's Step 2)
- Do NOT start problem-solving (that's Step 3)
- Use reflective listening, not interrogation
- Pursue the child's concern until it is specific and clear
- Expect the first answer to be vague — drill deeper

### Step 2: Define the Adult's Concern

**Goal:** Put your concern on the table alongside (not above) the child's.

**How:**
- "The thing is..." or "My concern is..."
- Keep it brief and concrete
- This is NOT a lecture. One or two sentences.

```
Parent: "The thing is, I'm concerned because you need time for dinner, 
         homework, and getting to bed on time. If tablet time runs long, 
         everything gets squeezed."
```

### Step 3: The Invitation — Collaborate on a Solution

**Goal:** Invite the child to brainstorm a solution that addresses BOTH concerns.

**How:**
- "I wonder if there's a way we can [child's concern] AND [parent's concern]."
- "Do you have any ideas for how we can solve this?"
- The solution must be **realistic, mutually satisfactory, and durable**

```
Parent: "I wonder if there's a way for you to not be interrupted in the 
         middle of something AND for us to still have time for dinner, 
         homework, and bed. Do you have any ideas?"
Child:  "What if you tell me 10 minutes before? So I can get to a save 
         point?"
Parent: "That could work. And what if we set the start time so there's 
         a natural stopping point before dinner?"
Child:  "Like start at 4:30 and you warn me at 5:20?"
Parent: "Let's try that. If it doesn't work, we'll come back and figure 
         out something else. Deal?"
Child:  "Deal."
```

**Testing the solution:**
- Does it address the child's concern? (not interrupted mid-game) YES
- Does it address the adult's concern? (enough time for evening routine) YES
- Is it realistic? YES
- Is it mutually satisfactory? (not one person caving) YES

## Plan B: Common Failures

### 1. Emergency Plan B (in the heat of the moment)

Sometimes you haven't done Proactive Plan B and the situation erupts NOW.

- Still follow the three steps, but more briefly
- "Hey, I can see this is really hard right now. What's going on?"
- Less ideal than proactive, but far better than Plan A

### 2. The Child Says "I Don't Know"

This is normal. It means:
- They need more time to think ("Take your time, I'm not in a rush")
- The question was too broad (make it more specific)
- They've been trained that their input doesn't matter (keep at it, trust builds over time)
- They genuinely don't know the words — help them: "Some kids tell me they feel [X]. Is that part of it?"

### 3. The Solution Falls Apart

Expected and OKAY. Go back to Plan B.
- "The plan we came up with isn't working so well. Can we revisit?"
- This is iteration, not failure

### 4. You Slip Into Plan A

Everyone does. Notice it, name it, reset.
- "I just told you what to do without hearing your side. Let me start over. What's hard about this for you?"

## CPS with Different Ages

**Ages 3-5:** Use simpler language, offer choices, keep Plan B conversations short. "What's hard about putting on shoes? Is it the laces? The time? You don't want to stop playing?"

**Ages 6-12:** Full Plan B process. Children this age can articulate concerns with scaffolding. Write unsolved problems on paper together.

**Ages 13-18:** Especially effective. Teens crave autonomy and respect. Plan B gives them voice. "I'm not going to impose a solution. I want to understand your side and find something that works for both of us."

---

## Anti-patterns

1. **Skipping the Empathy Step** — jumping to solutions is the #1 failure mode
2. **Using Plan B as camouflaged Plan A** — "Let's collaborate... but here's what we're going to do"
3. **Vague unsolved problems** — "has anger issues" instead of "difficulty transitioning from screen time"
4. **Attributing misbehavior to motivation** — "He could if he wanted to" is the old lens
5. **Working on too many problems at once** — prioritize 2-3, Plan C the rest
6. **Doing Plan B when flooded** — if you or the child is in meltdown, wait. Stabilize first.
7. **Expecting immediate results** — the first Plan B conversation may not produce a solution. That's normal.
8. **Abandoning Plan C as "giving up"** — it's strategic, temporary, and essential
9. **Lecturing during the Define step** — keep it to 1-2 sentences

## Output Format

When analyzing a situation through CPS:

```
## CPS Analysis

**Situation:** [brief description]

### ALSUP Assessment
- **Likely lagging skills:** [list with brief explanation]
- **Unsolved problem (specific):** [formatted as "Difficulty [doing X] in [context]"]

### Plan Selection
- **Plan A (impose):** [why this would/wouldn't work here]
- **Plan B (collaborate):** [recommended — with outline]
- **Plan C (drop it):** [if applicable, what to table for now]

### Plan B Dialogue Script
**Empathy Step:**
[Parent-child dialogue — gathering the child's concern]

**Define Step:**
[Parent states their concern in 1-2 sentences]

**Invitation Step:**
[Collaborative brainstorming toward mutual solution]

### Solution Test
- Child's concern addressed? [yes/no — how]
- Adult's concern addressed? [yes/no — how]
- Realistic and durable? [yes/no]
```
