# Collaborative & Proactive Solutions (CPS) Skill

> "Kids do well if they can. If they can't, something is getting in the way — and our job is to figure out what that is." — Ross Greene

> Background and theory: Read references/foundation.md

## Plan B: The Three Steps

Plan B is done **proactively** (not in the heat of the moment). Problems are PREDICTABLE.

### Step 1: Empathy — Gather Information

**Goal:** Understand the child's concern about the unsolved problem.

```
Parent: "I've noticed that when it's time to turn off the tablet, things 
         get really hard. What's up with that?"
Child:  "I don't want to stop!"
Parent: "Can you tell me more? What makes it hard to stop?"
Child:  "I'm always in the middle of something."
Parent: "So you feel interrupted. Is there more?"
Child:  "Yeah, and I never know when it's coming. You just say 'time's up' 
         and I haven't saved my game."
Parent: "So it feels sudden, and you lose progress. Anything else?"
```

**Critical rules:**
- Do NOT skip or rush. Most adults fail here.
- Do NOT insert your concern (that's Step 2)
- Do NOT problem-solve (that's Step 3)
- Expect the first answer to be vague — drill deeper

### Step 2: Define the Adult's Concern

Put your concern alongside (not above) the child's. Brief and concrete.

```
Parent: "The thing is, I'm concerned because you need time for dinner, 
         homework, and getting to bed on time."
```

### Step 3: The Invitation — Collaborate

Invite brainstorming for a solution that addresses BOTH concerns.

```
Parent: "I wonder if there's a way for you to not be interrupted AND 
         for us to still have time for everything else. Any ideas?"
Child:  "What if you tell me 10 minutes before?"
Parent: "That could work. And what if we set a start time so there's 
         a natural stopping point?"
Child:  "Like start at 4:30 and you warn me at 5:20?"
Parent: "Let's try that. If it doesn't work, we'll figure out 
         something else."
```

**Solution test:** Child's concern addressed? Adult's concern addressed? Realistic? Mutually satisfactory?

## Common Failures and Fixes

### "I Don't Know"
- Give more time: "Take your time, I'm not in a rush"
- Make it more specific
- Help: "Some kids tell me they feel [X]. Is that part of it?"

### Solution Falls Apart
Go back to Plan B. "The plan isn't working. Can we revisit?" This is iteration, not failure.

### Emergency Plan B (in the moment)
"Hey, I can see this is really hard right now. What's going on?" Less ideal but far better than Plan A.

### Slipping into Plan A
Notice, name, reset: "I just told you what to do without hearing your side. Let me start over."

---

## Anti-patterns

1. **Skipping the Empathy Step** — #1 failure mode
2. **Plan B as camouflaged Plan A** — "Let's collaborate... but here's what we're doing"
3. **Vague unsolved problems** — "has anger issues" instead of specific situations
4. **Attributing to motivation** — "He could if he wanted to" is the old lens
5. **Too many problems at once** — prioritize 2-3, Plan C the rest
6. **Plan B when flooded** — stabilize first
7. **Expecting immediate results** — first conversation may not produce a solution
8. **Abandoning Plan C as giving up** — it's strategic and temporary
9. **Lecturing during Define step** — keep it to 1-2 sentences
