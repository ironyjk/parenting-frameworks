# Understanding by Design (UbD) Skill

> "The best designs derive backward from the learnings sought." — Grant Wiggins & Jay McTighe

## Overview

Understanding by Design (UbD) is a curriculum planning framework that reverses the traditional approach of starting with activities and textbooks. Instead, it begins with the end in mind: What should students understand? How will we know they understand? Only then do we plan the learning activities. This "backward design" ensures alignment between goals, assessments, and instruction.

---

## The Three Stages of Backward Design

### Stage 1: Identify Desired Results

**What should students know, understand, and be able to do?**

Four levels of priority (nested circles):

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Worth being familiar with                    │
│  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐ │
│  │ Important to know and do                │ │
│  │  ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐│ │
│  │  │ Enduring Understanding (Big Ideas)  ││ │
│  │  │  ┌─────────────────────────────────┐││ │
│  │  │  │ Transfer Goals                  │││ │
│  │  │  └─────────────────────────────────┘││ │
│  │  └─────────────────────────────────────┘│ │
│  └─────────────────────────────────────────┘ │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Big Ideas** — The core, transferable concepts that anchor the unit:
- Have enduring value beyond the classroom
- Reside at the heart of the discipline
- Require "uncoverage" (they are abstract, often counterintuitive)
- Offer potential for engaging students

**Examples of Big Ideas:**
- "Correlation does not imply causation" (statistics)
- "Every story has a perspective, and perspective shapes meaning" (literature)
- "Form follows function" (biology, design, architecture)
- "Models are useful lies — they simplify reality to reveal patterns" (science)

**Essential Questions** — Open-ended questions that provoke inquiry and point toward Big Ideas:
- Have no single "right" answer
- Raise other important questions
- Recur naturally across contexts
- Are worth revisiting over time

| Topical Question (closed) | Essential Question (open) |
|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| "What were the causes of WWI?" | "Why do nations go to war? Is war inevitable?" |
| "What is photosynthesis?" | "How do organisms get the energy they need to survive?" |
| "What is the formula for area?" | "How do we measure what cannot be directly counted?" |

### Stage 2: Determine Acceptable Evidence

**How will we know students understand?**

Design assessments BEFORE planning lessons. Two types:

**Performance Tasks** — Complex, authentic tasks that require students to apply knowledge in realistic contexts. A performance task has:
- **Goal:** A real-world problem or challenge
- **Role:** The student plays a specific role (scientist, advisor, designer)
- **Audience:** A defined audience beyond the teacher
- **Situation:** A realistic context with constraints
- **Product/Performance:** A tangible deliverable
- **Standards:** Clear criteria for success (rubric)

This is captured in the **GRASPS** framework:

| Element | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **G**oal | What must be accomplished | Recommend a nutrition plan |
| **R**ole | Who the student is in the scenario | Health consultant |
| **A**udience | Who the student addresses | A family with specific dietary needs |
| **S**ituation | The context and constraints | Limited budget, cultural preferences |
| **P**roduct | What the student creates | Written plan + presentation |
| **S**tandards | Criteria for judgment | Nutritional accuracy, feasibility, clarity |

**Other Evidence:**
- Quizzes and tests (knowledge and skill)
- Academic prompts (short constructed responses)
- Observations and dialogues
- Self-assessments and reflections

### Stage 3: Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction

**What activities will equip students to demonstrate understanding?**

Only NOW do you plan activities, and you plan them to directly serve Stages 1 and 2.

## The Six Facets of Understanding

Wiggins and McTighe define understanding not as a single thing but as six interrelated capacities. True understanding is demonstrated when a student can:

| Facet | Description | Prompt |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| **Explain** | Provide thorough, supported accounts of phenomena | "Why does this happen? How does it work?" |
| **Interpret** | Make meaning through narrative, translation, metaphor | "What does this mean? What is the story behind it?" |
| **Apply** | Use knowledge effectively in new, diverse contexts | "How and when can I use this?" |
| **Perspective** | See from different viewpoints, critically analyze | "From whose perspective? What are the alternatives?" |
| **Empathize** | Perceive sensitively, walk in another's shoes | "What would it feel like to be in their position?" |
| **Self-Knowledge** | Recognize one's own ignorance, biases, habits of mind | "What are my blind spots? How does my experience shape my view?" |

**Assessment implication:** A robust assessment plan targets multiple facets, not just Explain and Apply (the two most commonly tested).

## WHERETO: Designing the Learning Plan

The WHERETO framework ensures the learning plan is coherent and student-centered:

| Element | Question | Design Action |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| **W** — Where and Why | Where is the unit going? Why? | Share learning goals and Essential Questions upfront |
| **H** — Hook and Hold | How will you hook and sustain interest? | Open with a provocative entry point, maintain curiosity |
| **E** — Equip and Enable | What experiences will equip students? | Provide knowledge, skills, and strategies needed |
| **R** — Reflect, Rethink, Revise | How will students reflect and revise? | Build in revision cycles, metacognition, self-assessment |
| **E** — Evaluate | How will students self-evaluate and be evaluated? | Formative checks, rubrics, peer review |
| **T** — Tailor | How will you tailor to individual needs? | Differentiate by readiness, interest, learning profile |
| **O** — Organize | How will the unit be sequenced? | Logical flow that builds understanding progressively |

## UbD Unit Design Template

### Stage 1 — Desired Results

```
Transfer Goals:
  Students will independently use their learning to...
  • [Long-term transfer goal]

Meaning:
  Understandings:                    Essential Questions:
  Students will understand that...   • [Open-ended question]
  • [Big Idea as a full sentence]    • [Open-ended question]
  • [Big Idea as a full sentence]

Acquisition:
  Knowledge:                         Skills:
  Students will know...              Students will be able to...
  • [Key facts, concepts, terms]     • [Specific, assessable skills]
```

### Stage 2 — Evidence

```
Performance Task(s):
  GRASPS:
  Goal:       [What must be accomplished]
  Role:       [Student's role]
  Audience:   [Who receives the product]
  Situation:  [Context and constraints]
  Product:    [What is created]
  Standards:  [Evaluation criteria]

Other Evidence:
  • [Quiz, test, prompt, observation, self-assessment]
```

### Stage 3 — Learning Plan

```
WHERETO sequence:
  W: [How goals and expectations are communicated]
  H: [Hook — entry event or provocative question]
  E: [Key learning activities and experiences]
  R: [Reflection, revision, rethinking opportunities]
  E: [Formative assessment checkpoints]
  T: [Differentiation strategies]
  O: [Sequence and organization]
```

## Common Misunderstandings About UbD

| Misunderstanding | Clarification |
|------------------|---------------|
| "UbD is just teaching to the test" | UbD designs authentic performance tasks, not standardized test prep |
| "Start with standards" | Standards inform Stage 1 but are not the same as Big Ideas. Standards are inputs; understanding is the goal |
| "Backward design means no room for flexibility" | The framework provides direction, not a script. Adjust activities based on student response |
| "Understanding = knowing" | Knowing is necessary but insufficient. Understanding requires transfer to new contexts |
| "One assessment is enough" | True understanding requires multiple forms of evidence across multiple facets |
| "Activities come first" | The most common design error. Activities must serve Stages 1 and 2, not the other way around |

## UbD Applied: Sample Unit Sketch

**Topic:** Nutrition and Health (Grade 6 Science)

**Stage 1:**
- **Big Idea:** "You are what you eat" — food choices directly affect body systems and long-term health
- **Essential Questions:** "How does what I eat affect how I feel and function?" / "Why do people make unhealthy food choices even when they know better?"
- **Transfer Goal:** Students will independently evaluate nutritional information and make informed food choices

**Stage 2:**
- **Performance Task (GRASPS):** You are a nutritionist (Role) hired by a local school (Audience) to redesign the cafeteria menu (Product) to be healthier while staying within budget and appealing to students (Situation). Present your plan with nutritional justification (Standards: accuracy, feasibility, persuasiveness).
- **Other Evidence:** Food diary analysis, nutrient identification quiz, reflection journal

**Stage 3 (WHERETO):**
- W: Share Essential Questions and performance task overview on Day 1
- H: Students analyze their own food diary from the past 3 days
- E: Nutrient lessons, food label reading, calorie/nutrient calculations
- R: Peer review of menu drafts, revision cycle
- E: Exit tickets on nutrient knowledge, self-assessment rubric
- T: Choice in presentation format, tiered complexity of menu constraints
- O: Build from personal → scientific → applied knowledge

## Anti-patterns

1. **Activity-first design** — Planning fun activities then retrofitting learning goals
2. **Coverage mentality** — Trying to "cover" everything instead of "uncovering" Big Ideas
3. **Assessment as afterthought** — Designing the test last, disconnected from goals
4. **Topical questions only** — Asking factual questions but not Essential Questions
5. **Single-facet assessment** — Testing only recall (Explain) without transfer (Apply) or perspective
6. **Ignoring Stage 2** — Jumping from Stage 1 goals directly to Stage 3 activities
7. **Confusing activity with learning** — "Students will do a poster" is not a learning goal
8. **Essential Questions that aren't essential** — Closed, factual, or too narrow to sustain inquiry
9. **One-shot design** — Not iterating on the design after teaching it
10. **Standards as understanding** — "Students will meet standard 4.2.1" is compliance, not understanding
